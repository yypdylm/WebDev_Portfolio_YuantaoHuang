Design Thinking Process for My Website

Design thinking is a human - centered approach to innovation that emphasizes empathy, ideation, and prototyping. Here's how I applied design thinking to my website:

1

Empathize

To understand the needs of my users, I conducted interviews and surveys with potential employers and peers. I asked about their expectations for a portfolio website, pain points in navigation, and preferred content formats. This helped me identify key user groups and their specific requirements.

Example: In one interview, a UX designer emphasized the importance of visual hierarchy and intuitive navigation for quick information access.

2

Define

Based on feedback, I defined the core problem: creating a portfolio that showcases technical skills while providing an accessible narrative of my design process. Key challenges included balancing visual appeal with content clarity and ensuring mobile responsiveness.

Example: I created user personas and journey maps to visualize common scenarios and identify areas for improvement.

3

Ideate

I explored various layout options through sketching and wireframing. Key ideas included modular content blocks, interactive elements for project details, and a consistent color scheme reflecting professionalism and creativity.

Example: I experimented with card-based layouts to present projects, allowing users to focus on specific work without information overload.

4

Prototype

I built a low-fidelity prototype using paper and digital tools to test navigation flow. This evolved into a high-fidelity prototype with interactive elements, allowing me to simulate user interactions and gather feedback on usability.

Example: The initial prototype revealed that users struggled to find my contact information, leading to its prominent placement in subsequent versions.

5

Test

User testing with 5 participants uncovered issues with mobile navigation and content hierarchy. I iterated on the design, simplifying menus and enhancing visual cues for important sections.

Example: A participant suggested adding hover effects to project cards, which I implemented to improve interaction and highlight clickable elements.
